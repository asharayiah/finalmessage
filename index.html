In Remembrance of the Birth of Muhammad (pbuh)

Friday • 29 Aug 2025 (solar) • 16 Rabi‘ al‑Awwal (lunar)

The lunar calendar is a sign. Moonlight is gentle, pure, cool; it guides without burning. Sunlight is fierce, a beam of many colors that powers our days. Both are gifts—two kinds of light, two measures of time. In honoring the Prophet’s birth by the lunar month, we remember a guidance that comes with mercy, steadiness, and night‑time clarity.

Muhammad (pbuh) was born an orphan. He knew the ache of those without protection, the hunger of those without provision. He grew into a man whose strength was moral strength—courage tied to compassion, justice tied to mercy. He stood with the poor, the travelers, the widowed, the captive, and the child. He taught that dignity is not for sale, that truth is not a costume, that worship without justice is empty, and justice without mercy is incomplete.

Religion, in Arabic, is dīn—a way of life and dealing: how we live, trade, argue, forgive, share water, protect the weak, and tell the truth when it costs us. The Final Message did not come to create tribes of superiority, but to summon one human family to God‑consciousness, responsibility, and hope.

So let this remembrance be practical: feed the hungry, free the oppressed, heal what is broken, spend of what you love, refuse to be entertained by cruelty, and let your knowledge serve life. If you have power, make it shelter. If you have a platform, make it a voice for those who tremble. If you have wealth, make it a bridge toward mercy.

O people of insight—believers, doubters, seekers, and thinkers: do not measure success only by heat and speed, but also by the cool clarity of a moon that crosses darkness without arrogance. The Prophet Muhammad (pbuh) was a mercy to all worlds. Be a proof of that mercy in your world, today.

From the Children of Gaza to the World — A Letter

We speak as children. We ask as children. We testify as children.

We are hungry. Our bread is memory. Our water is a promise adults keep postponing. Do not tell us patience while our stomachs argue with the night.

We are cold. The wind knows our names because our walls are gone. If you cannot rebuild our houses today, rebuild our warmth with your hands and your courage.

We are afraid. Fear is a teacher too early for children. Let your voice be the door that fear cannot pass.

We do not ask you to be perfect. We ask you to be human. Write to your leaders. Refuse the lie that some lives are worth less. Make space at your table. Share your power. Stand between us and the harm that keeps walking. Let your faith, your philosophy, your science and your art join hands for life.

Do not let eternity record your silence. If history is a court, be a witness for mercy. If God is watching, be an answer, not an excuse. If you have children, see us as your own. If you have none, imagine that you do—and answer as if their names were ours.
